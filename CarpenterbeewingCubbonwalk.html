Carpenter bees are one of the creatures I was ignorant about until I found one in Cubbon Park. Why are they called carpenter bees? Because they burrow into wood. And in Cubbon Park, you can see their nest on the wooden sculptures near the bandstand. I see them every other day while commuting to and from work. When I first saw them, I mistook them for a beetle, but the eyes, antennae, and legs reminded me of honey bees. But it was the wings that caught my attention. The shiny iridescent wing was mesmerising. From different angles, it shows different shades of purple, blue, and green. I knew it was something called structural colour, but I wasn’t sure what kind of structure it was. So, recently at work, we were asked to work on a nature walk in Cubbon Park with a focus on physics. And carpenter bees were on the top of my list. I wanted to talk about their wing colour. And their wing colour is due to one of my favourite physical phenomena, interference and soap bubbles. So, what is interference? It is when a wave overlaps with another wave of the same wavelength that their crests and valleys line up; they are in-phase, then they will get amplified, also called constructive interference, but if they are out-of-phase, they will cancel each other out, and it’s called destructive interference. To help with understanding the phenomenon better, you can play around with the small interactive widget made with the help of Claude.

How does that happen with carpenter bee wings? Luckily, I found a paper answering this question. It was by Stavenga et al. in 2023, looking at Xylocopa latipes (the genus name Xylocopa means “wood-cutter”), which is a carpenter bee species found in Southeast Asia. The one we have in Cubbon Park might be Xylocopa tenuiscapa. In the paper, they have an electron micrograph showing the cross-section of the carpenter bee wing. Carpenter bee wings are made up of chitin, a strong polysaccharide that makes up the exoskeleton of all insects. The carpenter bee wing looks dark because of the pigment called melanin. The micrograph shows distinct striations, approximately 100 nm thick, with different regions containing varying amounts of melanin. When the melanin content of chitin changes, the refractive index also changes. This means the wings have sheets or thin films of varying refractive indices stacked on top of each other. A very common thin film is a soap bubble or a drop of oil on a puddle of water after rain, which also iridesces, and the carpenter bee wing can be thought of as made of a stack of really durable soap bubbles on top of each other.

 

So, how does interference happen here? When the light falls on the wing at oblique angles, it can be reflected from the top surface of the wing, and some might go through and get reflected from any of the underlying layers. Since the light travels different distances through the wing, there will be a phase shift in the outgoing rays, which, if they are in phase, the intensity increases, and if they are out of phase, they’ll cancel each other out. Whether a light wave undergoes constructive or destructive interference depends on the thickness of the thin film. It should be comparable to that of the wavelength of light; if it is too thin or too thick, you won’t observe iridescence.
Next time, when you see a carpenter bee with shiny wings, you can explain to your friends why.
